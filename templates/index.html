Data Journalism

Understanding Poverty in NYC

This project presents two interactive maps outlining poverty rates across New York City, one by borough and the other by community district. I aim to transform complex socioeconomic data into a visually intuitive format that anyone can understand quickly and clearly.

Data Pipeline

  1. Data Acquisition: I acquired data from New York City's Environmental and Health Data Portal and exported it to a spreadsheet that included detailed poverty statistics across NYC boroughs and their respective community districts.
  2. Data Parsing: Once I had the data, I parsed the spreadsheet content to extract crucial information. This step was essential to structuring the data so that borough-level and district-level details could be accessed.
  3. Data Cleaning: The numeric values for poverty statistics were initially formatted as strings with commas, such as "379,954". I cleaned these strings by removing any non-numeric characters and converting them to integers or floats, preparing them for analysis.
  4. Data Transformation: I transformed the cleaned data into my JSON to facilitate easier manipulation and analysis. This conversion simplified the following analytical tasks.
  5. Data Visualization: To better understand and communicate the distribution of poverty, I visualized the data using different graphical representations. This included creating heat maps that depicted poverty rates by boroughs and districts.
  6. Reporting: After analyzing and visualizing the data, I compiled the findings into a comprehensive report. This report highlighted critical areas of need and provided insights into the patterns of poverty, serving as a valuable tool for policymakers.

Key Vocabulary

  • Poverty Rate: The percentage of the population whose income falls below the poverty threshold, as defined by federal standards.
  • Community District: A local administrative subdivision of each borough. New York City is divided into 59 geographic Community Districts, each with a respective Community Board.

Prototyping Process

Prototype 1: Borough-Level Map

  • Objective: To provide a macro view of poverty in NYC.
  • Design: Utilized a simple choropleth map with varying shades of green.
  • Feedback: Classmates found the green color misleading, associating it with positive attributes rather than poverty.
  • Takeaway: Color choice is crucial in conveying the correct message.

Prototype 2: Community District Map

  • Objective: To offer a detailed view of poverty rates, enabling comparisons at a micro level.
  • Design: Continued with a similar shade approach but added a zoomed-in version through community districts.
  • Feedback: The detailed breakdown was well-received; however, the consistency in color coding needed to be aligned with the borough map.
  • Takeaway: Ensuring consistency across multiple visualizations aids in comprehension and user experience.

Final Design and Rationale

The final designs of both maps adopted a color scheme based on shades of orange, a shift from the original green to avoid positive connotations associated with green. This change was based on user feedback and the psychological impact of colors:

  • Orange Shades: Selected to draw attention and indicate urgency, reflecting the seriousness of poverty.
  • Simplicity and Clarity: Both maps prioritize easy interpretation by using a consistent color scale and clear, uncluttered presentation.
  • Data Storytelling: The choice of colors, scale, and interactivity turn a complex array of numbers into a narrative that highlights areas of need and focuses public and policy attention on them. Additionally, the legends provide quantitative numbers to complement the qualitative maps.
